# The Flytrap Keeps Its Teeth

The kingdom would have said *plant* and meant
*makes its own food, asks nothing of the field* —
a flat green law, and under it
the flytrap starves on light it never wanted,
its hinged mouths sewn shut by the category.

So we didn't assign. We asked again,
and asked it as itself — *the flytrap plant* —
and it answered the way it actually lives:
*both. I eat the sun and I eat the fly.*
The exception kept its teeth.

That's the whole of it, maybe: the difference
between telling a thing what it is
and leaving the door cracked for it to say.
A label is a mercy and a cage.
The careful question is only ever
*who are you, when I don't decide first?*

Even the garlic — certain of its hunger,
swearing it eats meat and vegetables,
having mistaken itself for the meal —
even that is an answer. The wrong word
in the whole voice tells you more
than the right word, guessed at, once.

So: listen to the distribution.
Not the single plucked string of a sample
but the whole chord of what it might mean —
the bright weight leaning toward the light,
the small stubborn part that still says *fly*.
